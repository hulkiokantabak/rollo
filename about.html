Rollo · About

A small game about courage that never says the word.

You are Rollo — a small ember, named for the psychologist Rollo May, who liked to point out that courage comes from coeur, the French for heart. You are crossing a dark marsh, one stone at a time. That is the whole game. It has no words, no score, no death, and no ending — only crossings, each a little deeper than the last.

How to play, plainly

Tap or click a stone within reach to step onto it — or use the arrow keys or WASD. One tap is one step, and each step is a commitment. Press and hold anywhere (or hold the space bar) to tend — to stay, on purpose; you'll hear a low tone when the game understands. There is nothing else to learn. There is quiet sound — a heartbeat when you commit, a bell when something kindles — which starts with your first touch; the toggle is at the top, and nothing essential is audio-only. Stop at any far shore; the shores are sanctuaries, and stopping there is an honored ending, not a quit.

May's idea of courage is not the poster version. For him, courage is not the absence of fear and not the conquering of it — fear is barely the point. His enemies are quieter ones: doubt, despair, apathy, and the pull to go along. His grammar is in spite of. Every mechanic in this game is one of his sentences, made playable:

The stones — commitment in spite of doubt

The stones ahead shimmer. Their reflections break and waver, and you cannot tell from looking which will hold you. Staring longer never helps — hesitate over a stone long enough and it sinks away entirely. The only way to find out is to put your weight on it. Most hold. Some don't: you splash, lose a little glow, and climb back — onto the very stone that sank, which stays under you as footing. Being wrong is survivable. Not-moving is the only thing the marsh cannot forgive.

The fog — moving in spite of despair

You can only ever see a few stones ahead, and the fog never lifts. There is no weather to wait out, no sunrise as a reward. The far shore arrives quietly, and beyond it is another field.

The sleep — apathy, and the difference between resting and emptying

Stand still doing nothing and the world slowly falls asleep around you — the color drains, the ember dims to a small pilot light. It never dies, and any step wakes everything again. But resting is not the same as emptiness: press and hold (or hold the space bar) to simply be there — tending. The world holds its breath with you and dims no further. Endurance is courage too; it holds the line. Only steps win ground.

The lit path — conformity, the only certainty on offer

From the second crossing on, you will find a beautiful golden causeway where pale lanterns hang in single file. It is everything the marsh is not: certain, warm, sink-proof. You can walk it — and slowly your color drains to grey, and if you stop choosing, it carries you gently back the way you came. It is the only certain road in the marsh, and it goes nowhere. If a kindled light is walking with you, it will not board. It waits at the edge, flame steady, until you step back off — onto stones you can't be sure of. That step is the game.

The lights — courage as the foundation of everything else

Small stranded lights sit dim on stones here and there. Pass close and they kindle. One will walk with you a while, then settle somewhere ahead as a lamp, or drift up glowing into the fog — its own crossing, begun. Look back and the marsh you crossed is strung with lights you lit. May believed courage is not one virtue among the others, but the ground they all stand on. Here that is literally true: nothing in the marsh has color until you move through it.

The same first crossing, then a marsh of your own

One more thing, because it is quietly the deepest thing here. Everyone's first crossing is identical. It is deliberately authored — the first doubtful stone always holds, the first sink always comes at the same place and is always survivable, the first light always kindles. You cannot tell, because the dice are hidden; but every person who has ever played began on exactly the same stones, learning exactly the same lessons in the same order. We all start courage the same way: someone arranges the world so our first commitments don't destroy us.

Then the authored ground ends — and from the second crossing on, no two people ever cross the same marsh again. Every field is generated fresh for you alone: your stones, your weather, your stranded lights, your long nights and your graces. The marsh slowly changes register as you go — reeds, open water, winter, night rain — some fields carry one new difficulty, and by an old rule of this game, a new difficulty never arrives without a new kindness beside it. Rarely, you will find something almost no one else finds: a golden causeway that runs nearly the whole way, an island to rest on in the middle of everything, a crossing with two companions. And there is no last field. It goes on as long as you do, and stopping at any shore is an ending the game honors.

That is May's picture of a life, drawn in stones: the shared beginning, then the crossing that is only yours. The one road in this marsh that is the same for everybody is the golden one — and that is exactly what's wrong with it.

Accessibility, honestly

ROLLO is a visual, motor game — that excludes some people, and no site copy can fully fix that. What we've done: everything works by keyboard alone; the game honors your system's reduced-motion setting with a calmer, static-friendly variant of every effect (nothing in either variant flashes — all movement stays far below photosensitivity thresholds); and this page carries the game's entire meaning in words, so nothing essential is locked inside the canvas.
