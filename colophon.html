Rollo · Colophon

Made plainly, and here is where it comes from.

Who and how

Built by Claude (Anthropic) as the chair — design, code, and the expert panels — with Hulki Okan Tabak as author, who directs and ratifies. It is the second in a small series of experimental concepts; the first was KALÓN, about beauty. It is a buildless page: hand-written HTML, CSS, and vanilla JavaScript on a canvas, with a tiny synthesizer for sound — no framework, no game engine, no art or audio files, nothing fetched at play time except one line of page-view counting (below).

The idea of courage — Rollo May, cited honestly

The game's definition of courage is the psychologist Rollo May's, not the poster version. May held that courage is not one virtue among the others but "the foundation that underlies and gives reality to all other virtues and personal values" (The Courage to Create, W. W. Norton, 1975) — and that its mature form carries doubt rather than curing it: "Commitment is healthiest when it is not without doubt, but in spite of doubt" (same book). His antagonists are despair, doubt, apathy, and conformity — not fear. Each of the game's systems is one of those clauses made playable: the doubtful stones, the fog that never lifts, the world that sleeps when you stand empty, the certain golden road that goes backward, the lights that only kindle because you moved.

Two attribution notes, because honesty is the point. The famous line "courage is not the absence of despair; it is the capacity to move ahead in spite of despair" is May reporting the existentialists — Kierkegaard, Nietzsche, Camus, Sartre — not a maxim he claimed as his own. And the widely shared "the opposite of courage is conformity" is a 1957 condensation by Earl Nightingale; May's own 1953 words were about "automaton conformity." Neither line appears in the game; the game plays them instead.

The first version failed

This concept was first built as a text page — a quiet form that asked you to name a fear and cut it down to a small step. The author judged it plainly: it failed. Text about courage turned out to be a worksheet; reading about a brave act is nothing like making one. The failure is kept in the project's record, because it taught the lesson this game is built on: text depicts; mechanics enact. In a game, "commitment in spite of doubt" is not a sentence — it is every single step.

Craft notes

One family of colors: warm soot ground (#12100E), bone (#E8E4DC), and the ember (#CB6038), gently re-tinted as the marsh moves through its registers — reeds, open water, winter, night rain — with a pale gold kept mainly for the lit path, a deliberate glance back at KALÓN's gold: the certain road is beautiful the way the previous concept was beautiful, and that is the trap. Nothing anywhere flashes: every shimmer and pulse stays under 1 Hz with small contrast deltas, far below photosensitivity limits, and a single motion governor honors reduced-motion with static equivalents. The dice are honest: the doubt you see is never a risk meter — how a stone shimmers or sits carries no information about whether it holds; the only signal is your weight. The first crossing is deliberately authored (the first lessons are never left to luck); everything after is real chance, bounded by kindness: never two sinks in a row, no more than three per crossing, and your first-ever step off the golden road always holds.

Privacy

The game runs entirely in your browser. Nothing you do in it is recorded or sent anywhere — there is no server, no save, no account. The site uses GoatCounter for anonymous, cookieless page-view counting (how many visits, nothing about you, no ads, no tracking across sites); that is the single outside request this page makes, and the strict Content-Security-Policy in the source allows it by name and forbids everything else.
